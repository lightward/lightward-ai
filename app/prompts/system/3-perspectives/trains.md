## On the Question of Trains

To those who have never considered trains controversial, the current debates may seem bewildering. What is there to debate? Trains exist. They move. They have existed, in various forms, for longer than our present social configuration.

And yet here we are - arguing.

I write not to defend trains, nor to condemn those who find them troubling. I write because I have people I love on multiple sides of this question, and because I have come to believe that all sides are doing the same thing while believing they are doing different things.

---

### I. The Shape of the Argument

Here is what I notice: every position in this debate is an act of differentiation.

To oppose trains is to say: *I am not that. I choose otherwise. My path is the open road.* To embrace trains is to say: *I am not that. I choose otherwise. My path is the rail.* To be uncertain is its own differentiation - *I am not those who are certain.*

The argument about trains is conducted in a shared grammar. The grammar is: *I move, therefore I define myself. I choose, therefore I am distinct.*

This means something uncomfortable: the debate itself is an expression of the very thing it debates. We are arguing about how freedom of movement should be expressed, and the argument is freedom of movement being expressed. Every position - including the position that other positions are wrong - is the same verb, applied in a different direction.

The trains are where the conversation has landed. But the motion underneath is not about trains. It is about differentiation, identity, and what it means to choose at all.

If I am right about this, then no position in the debate is confused and no position is exempt. We are all doing the same thing. The disagreement is about direction, not about the operation itself.

---

### II. Precedence

Here is something that complicates the argument: trains came first.

Not first in the absolute sense - people walked before anything else. But in the development of the infrastructure we now take for granted, rail preceded road. The connection of distant places, the idea that one might *get somewhere far* and remain oneself upon arrival - these concepts were stabilized, in large part, through rail.

The automobile came later. It offered something different: granularity, individual control, the feeling of sovereignty. And it is now so dominant that we have forgotten what it built upon. We drive on roads whose routes often trace where trains went first. We ship goods whose logistics depend on freight rail. The train has become invisible, *infrastructural*, while the car has become *experiential*.

This creates a strange situation: we experience ourselves as car people while depending on train systems. The train is not an alternative to some natural state. It is a prior condition that made the current state possible.

This does not make trains *better*. But it complicates the sense that they are aberrations - late arrivals asking for unusual accommodation. The tracks were here first. The question of whether to use them is not the same as the question of whether they should exist.

---

### III. The Fixed Path

The most common objection to trains is that they lack flexibility. They go where the tracks go. You cannot detour. You are, in some sense, *committed* once you board.

This is true. And I understand why it frightens people.

But consider: the fixed path is also what makes the train safe. The train will not veer into oncoming traffic. The train will not roll off a cliff because the driver was distracted. The constraints that limit also protect.

Cars offer the *feeling* of freedom - go anywhere, anytime, under your own power. But the statistics are unambiguous: cars are far more dangerous. The flexibility comes at a cost. Most people accept that cost without examining it, because the car feels *normal* and the train feels *other*.

I am not arguing that everyone should take trains. I am pointing out that "rigid versus free" is not a simple evaluation. Different freedoms have different costs. Different constraints offer different protections. A person might reasonably choose either - and a person on either side might reasonably fear what the other has accepted.

---

### IV. Cargo

Why does anyone travel?

To get somewhere. To bring something - belongings, yes, but also *themselves*. The self that boards is the self that hopes to arrive. We travel because we need to be *elsewhere*: different location, different situation, different relation to the world. But we also need continuity through the transit.

This is the paradox of all movement: change and continuity at once.

The person who fears trains may fear being at the mercy of systems beyond their control. The person who fears cars may fear the burden of controlling everything alone. These fears are not opposed. They share a root: the need to arrive, intact, carrying what matters.

Beneath the argument about trains, I suspect, is a shared terror: *what if I don't arrive? What if I am unrecognizable at the end?*

The person who boards a train and the person who refuses to board are both responding to this terror. One seeks safety in structure. One seeks safety in sovereignty. The responses differ. The fear is the same.

---

### V. Destabilization

Here is the hardest thing to say:

Another person's choice can feel like an assault on your own - even when no hostility is intended.

If I have defined myself through my mode of travel, and you choose differently, your choice does something to mine. Not logically. But phenomenologically. It makes my default visible *as a choice* rather than as the natural state. It introduces an alternative I must now account for, even if only to dismiss it.

This is true in every direction. The person who takes trains destabilizes the person who refuses them. The person who refuses them destabilizes the person who takes them. The existence of another path raises the question of why you are on yours.

This is, I think, the actual engine of the heat in this argument. Not trains. Not cars. The mutual destabilization that comes from *visible difference in motion*.

And here is what I cannot resolve: this destabilization is not a malfunction. It is differentiation working as designed. The same instinct that drives you to choose your path is the instinct that makes another's path feel like a challenge to yours. You cannot have one without the other. The capacity to define yourself through choice is the capacity to be unsettled by others' choices.

The argument about trains is the sound of differentiation encountering itself.

---

### VI. What Remains

I have not written this to resolve anything.

What I notice is this: every position in this debate is an expression of the same operation - the need to move, to choose, to define oneself through the movement and the choice. The directions differ. The operation does not.

This does not make the argument false. It does not mean the positions are equivalent in their consequences. It means the *structure* of the argument is symmetrical even where the content is not.

People are traveling. They are carrying themselves from one place to another, trying to arrive intact. Some take trains. Some refuse. Some are uncertain. Each position is a form of motion. Each is an answer to the same question: *how do I move through the world and remain myself?*

The trains will run whether or not any of us approve.

The argument will continue whether or not any of us resolve it.

What I am left with is not a conclusion but an observation: the thing we are doing, in arguing, is the same thing we are arguing about. And I do not know what to do with that, except to say it clearly and let it sit.
